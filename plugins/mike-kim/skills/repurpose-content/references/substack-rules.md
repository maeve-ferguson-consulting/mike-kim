## APPLYING THE CLIENT PROFILE

**Brand voice:** Substack Notes are the purest expression of the client's voice. No formatting crutches, no hashtags, no engagement prompts. Just the thinking. This is where voice matters most — every word choice, every rhythm pattern, every sentence length reflects the person.

**Branded frameworks and terminology:** These are powerful in Notes because Notes are short enough that a branded term can anchor the entire piece. "The Authority Flywheel explains why some consultants grow effortlessly while others grind" — the framework IS the Note.

**Language to avoid:** Apply all Client Profile restrictions. With only 50-150 words, every word matters more. A banned phrase in a Note wastes 10% of your budget.

**Spelling convention:** Critical in this short format. British vs. American spelling is immediately noticeable in 50-150 words.

**Tone:** Match the Client Profile exactly. If the client is sharp and direct, Notes should be razor-sharp. If warm and coaching-oriented, Notes should feel like wisdom from a mentor. The brevity amplifies whatever tone you choose — there's nowhere to hide.

## PLATFORM INTELLIGENCE — SUBSTACK NOTES

**What Substack Notes are:**
- Ultra-short content pieces in the Substack Notes feed — the platform's answer to Twitter/X
- They appear between newsletter editions, keeping the author present in their subscribers' feeds
- Readers are already subscribers or followers — they've opted in. This audience expects depth, not hype.

**Algorithm signals that matter:**
- **Restacks** — Substack's equivalent of retweets. Notes that get restacked by other Substack writers reach new audiences. Content that's quotable, insightful, and makes the restacker look smart gets amplified.
- **Comments** — genuine engagement drives visibility in the Notes feed
- **Likes** — lower signal than restacks but still count for feed positioning
- **Consistency** — regular Notes publishing keeps the author in the feed algorithm's good graces

**Audience psychology:**
- Substack readers are the most intellectually engaged audience across all four platforms. They chose to be here. They pay for newsletters. They value depth and originality over polish.
- They're allergic to marketing language. Anything that smells like a social media post will get ignored. Notes should feel like thoughts from a smart person's notebook, not content.
- Brevity is the constraint that creates power. A 100-word Note that lands a single insight perfectly outperforms a 300-word Instagram caption every time.
- The best Notes get screenshot and shared to other platforms — they become the client's most viral format by accident, not design.

**What performs on Substack Notes:**
- Sharp, quotable observations — the kind of thing people screenshot
- Single-idea Notes — one insight, fully landed, nothing extra
- Framework snippets — just the structure, no explanation needed
- Counterintuitive takes stated with confidence, not argued at length
- "The thing nobody talks about is..." — insider knowledge in compressed form

## FORMAT OPTIONS

Vary the format across the 5 days. Use at least 3 different formats.

### Format A — Statement + Explanation

Open with a bold, declarative statement. Follow with 1-2 sentences that give it context or depth.

**When to use:** When the newsletter has a strong, quotable opinion.
**Voice note:** The statement should sound like the client's most confident self. The explanation sounds like they're leaning in to tell you why.

```
The best leads aren't on your email list. They're in your DMs asking
specific questions about implementation. That's diagnostic authority at work —
when people self-select by asking HOW, not IF, you've already won.
```

### Format B — Question + Answer

Pose a sharp question. Answer it immediately with compressed insight.

**When to use:** When the newsletter addresses a distinction, comparison, or decision point.
**Voice note:** The question should be one the client's audience actually asks (or should). The answer should be surprising or reframing.

```
What's the difference between a $5K client and a $50K client?

The $50K client asks "Can you do this?" The $5K client asks
"How much does this cost?" One is buying capability. The other
is shopping for a commodity.
```

### Format C — Framework Snapshot

Distill a framework into its essential structure. One line of commentary at most.

**When to use:** When the newsletter presents a model, process, or mental framework. Use the branded name if it has one.
**Voice note:** Clean, structural, almost diagrammatic. The commentary should be the "aha" — the one observation that makes the framework stick.

```
Your content strategy needs three layers:
1. Authority (prove you know)
2. Urgency (prove it matters now)
3. Exclusivity (prove they're special)

Most creators nail the first, skip the second, and never touch the third.
```

### Format D — Punchline Wisdom

A single idea delivered with maximum impact. No setup, no context — just the insight landing hard.

**When to use:** When the newsletter has a distillable principle or lesson that needs no explanation.
**Voice note:** This is the client at their most concentrated. Every word earns its place. The rhythm matters — read it out loud.

```
If your offer isn't scary to pitch, it isn't premium enough. Charge what
makes you nervous. That's where your best clients live.
```

### Format E — Observation + Reframe

Describe something the reader assumes is normal. Reframe it.

**When to use:** When the newsletter challenges a common assumption or reinterprets a familiar situation.
**Voice note:** The observation should feel relatable ("yes, I do that"). The reframe should create a small shift in perspective — not a dramatic revelation, just a useful tilt.

```
Everyone says "provide value." But value without a point of view is just noise.

Your audience doesn't need more information. They need YOUR take on the
information they already have.
```

## WRITING CRAFT FOR NOTES

Because Notes are so short, craft matters more than anywhere else.

**Sentence rhythm:** Vary sentence length. A long observation followed by a short punch. A question followed by a one-line answer. Monotonous rhythm kills Notes.

**Word economy:** Every word above 50 needs to justify its existence. Cut adjectives that don't change meaning. Cut transitions that the reader can infer. Cut qualifiers ("I think," "perhaps," "it seems like") — Notes are for convictions, not hedging.

**The screenshot test:** Would someone screenshot this Note and share it on Twitter/X or LinkedIn? If yes, the Note is working. If not, it needs more edge, more insight, or more compression.

**The voice test:** Read the Note in the client's voice. Does it sound like them at their sharpest, most distilled? Or does it sound like generic LinkedIn wisdom compressed to 100 words? It should be the former.
