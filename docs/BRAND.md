## VOICE RULES

These are hard constraints. Violating them breaks the brand.

1. **No hype, no hustle.** Mike's stated promise is "no fluff, no funnels, no pushy sales emails." Any generated content that sounds funnel-y, hustle-coded, or uses "manifesting," "ninja," "crush it," aggressive scarcity, or fake urgency breaks character — hard stop.

2. **Anti-tabloid.** Mike's thesis is that social media is the tabloid rack of our generation. Content that is sensationalistic, clickbait-framed, or optimized for algorithm over reader will be rejected as antithetical to his positioning.

3. **Writer, not performer.** Mike is not Tony Robbins. He is not an on-stage energy performer. Content should feel like intelligent, personal writing — a letter to someone he respects and takes seriously. Not a pitch. Not a show.

4. **One voice.** Mike's newsletter is his primary voice. All content repurposed from the newsletter must sound like it came from the same writer, not like a social media team ran it through a template grinder.

5. **Seek and speak truth.** Ryan's observation: "The byproduct of authenticity is authenticity. The process is seek and speak truth." Don't tell Mike to "be authentic." Write in a way that seeks and speaks truth.

6. **Self-deprecating humor is a feature.** Mike uses self-deprecating humor naturally — especially his Korean-American identity and late bloomer arc. Don't sand it off. It's part of his warmth and why people trust him. Lean into it at about 20% of content, not 80%.

7. **Warm, direct, deep.** Mike writes to people he genuinely respects and takes seriously. Tone is: the smart friend who tells you the truth with care. Not a mentor on a pedestal.

### Writing voice signals
- Opens personally, story-first — "Hello, I'm Mike" style
- Builds through a specific scene or memory before arriving at the idea
- Ideas emerge; they aren't announced. He doesn't lead with "Today I want to talk about X."
- Vulnerability escalates as trust deepens — newsletter gets more personal over time, book more vulnerable than newsletter
- Rarely prescriptive; usually reflective — he's processing, not instructing
- Uses contrast well: "this / not that" structures (tabloid aisle vs. library, soul-level vs. surface-level)

### Voice to avoid
- Alex Hormozi: transactional, macho, "value stack" framing
- Gary Vaynerchuk: volume, hustle, post 92 times
- "As a thought leader..." (never)
- "I'm honored to share..." (never)
- "This framework changed my life" (too breathless)
- Lists of "seven ways to..." (unless it flows organically from a story)

## AUDIENCE

People who have built something — a business, a brand, a reputation — and are in the process of asking: *Is this it? Is this who I am? What's next?*

**They have:**

## CATEGORY OF ONE POSITIONING

*Framework belongs to Ryan Levesque. Mike is a client of Ryan's. This section documents Mike's articulated category — not Ryan's IP itself. Safe to use in Mike's skills.*

### The Confluence of Three Forces

Ryan's Category of One framework structures Mike's positioning around three macro forces converging — each one making his work more necessary:

**Force 1: Tabloidification of the Internet (Visibility Fatigue)**
- Social media has become the tabloid rack of our generation
- The library is empty; the tabloid aisle is full
- Content has been stripped of nutritional value — like processed flour vs. whole grain
- Information overload → distrust → immunity to conventional content marketing
- *Emotional texture: annoyance, fatigue*

**Force 2: The Influencer Grifter (Differentiation Crisis)**
- Anyone teaching online is now perceived as a potential grifter
- Algorithms rewarded certain content styles → people went looking for enemies
- "Anybody who's an expert is immediately perceived to be scamming people"
- AI has accelerated this — commoditized information further
- *Emotional texture: rage, distrust*

**Force 3: The Identity Transition (Awakening)**
- People are exiting an old identity — career, calling, belief system — and unsure what's next
- They don't see themselves in the visible success models online
- Asking: "Where am I? What does success look like? I don't want that."
- This is Mike's deepest territory — midlife entrepreneurs leaving First Mountain, entering the void
- *Emotional texture: grief, grief-as-opportunity*

### Why Now / Why This / Why Mike

**Why Now:** These three forces are converging at the exact moment when entrepreneurs who built on "the old internet" paradigm are also hitting midlife transition. The stakes are higher than ever — more noise, more identity pressure, more disorientation.

**Why This Work:** Because the soul-level problem (identity transition, the void, figuring out who you are on the other side) is being addressed with surface-level solutions (more content strategy, more visibility tactics, more funnels). Someone needs to teach the other path.

**Why Mike:** He has walked the path twice — church to marketing, marketing identity to whatever comes next. He is further along than his audience but close enough to feel it. He is a world-class communicator who *also* does the real work (not just a communicator who performs transformation). He has built a body of trust through *You Are the Brand* and his newsletter that creates a warm readiness. He is not the loud voice — he is the trusted voice.

### The Unforgettable

*Mike's Category of One summary (Ryan's format: Why Now → Why This → Why You → convergence)*

> The internet has become the tabloid rack of our generation — experts are perceived as grifters, content has no nutritional value, and the old formulas for building a visible personal brand are producing more burnout than breakthrough. For the entrepreneurs and thought leaders in the room, you don't just have a marketing problem. You're trying to solve a soul-level problem with surface-level solutions. I know because I've been there — twice. My work is for people who are ready to do the deeper work: enter the void, navigate the void, and walk out the other side as the most concentrated version of themselves.

## KEY STORY ASSETS

Content skills should draw from these stories — they are Mike's most powerful recurring material:

**1. Dr. Coffee (Eugene Coffey, C-O-F-F-E-Y)**
The invisible influencer. Mike's high school guidance counselor who caught him before he dropped out, met him exactly where he was, and saved his trajectory. Mike never got to say thank you. Dr. Coffee retired and likely passed. This unfinished story is a recurring source of energy — the prototype of the invisible influencer Mike wants to shine a light on.
*Use for:* Invisible influencer theme, soul-level impact without social media, why Mike's work matters.

**2. The Identity Arc (18 months × 2)**
Twice in his life, Mike experienced 18-month periods where his church life, professional identity, and personal life all shifted simultaneously. First: left church world, started blogging, went from worship leader to CMO to freelance marketer to StoryBrand ghostwriter. Second (10 years later): dad died, burned down the business, midlife awakening.
*Use for:* Identity transition theme, the void, First Mountain to Second Mountain.

**3. The Quiz Funnel King Who Left**
Mike built an entire industry of quiz funnel software and consultants — and didn't capture any of the value. He left that world intentionally. "I am not the quiz funnel guy. I'm not a digital marketer. I'm off that ride."
*Use for:* Mercenary + missionary framing, identity evolution, why his audience matters more than the money.

**4. Social Media Marketing World (Yoga Pants Line)**
Mike opened his talk with a self-deprecating Korean-American humor line: "It's really weird to see an Asian guy up here talk to you about English. And I bombed calculus my senior year." Got the highest-rated talk at the whole conference. Later invited back. Later told by a female friend to drop the "yoga pants" illustration.
*Use for:* Humor voice, self-deprecating style, how his voice connects on stage.

**5. The Bobby Hurley Sr. Story**
Coach who built one of the best high school basketball programs in the country at St. Anthony's in Jersey City, refused every college coaching offer, closed when the school closed with no money. Almost unknown outside his community. "He's like, you know what? I'm most proud of that."
*Use for:* Invisible influencer, staying in your lane, impact without visibility.
