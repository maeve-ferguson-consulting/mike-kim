# Ghostwriting Traps — Detailed Checks

Six universal anti-patterns that make ghostwritten content lose a person's voice. Check the draft against each one before presenting.

---

## 1. The Compression Trap

**What happens:** The writer takes a long, textured personal section and compresses it into one polished paragraph. The mess disappears — but the mess is where personality lives.

**Check:** Find every personal section in the draft. Is it at least 2-3 paragraphs? Does it include specific details, not just summaries? Does it breathe?

**Fix:** Expand. Add back the specific odd details from the raw material: plans that didn't happen, mundane moments, the actual sequence of events. Check the Voice Profile — if anti-patterns mention "needs texture" or "the mess matters," give it room.

**Red flag sentence:** "It was a week that didn't go as planned." (This is a summary. What specifically didn't go as planned?)

---

## 2. The Front-Load Trap

**What happens:** All the personal life material appears in the opening, then disappears completely. The rest reads like a different person wrote it.

**Check:** Does the personal thread return after the opening? Is there a moment in the middle or close where the personal connects back to the strategic?

**Fix:** Weave personal references throughout. The personal thread should return at least once after the opening. It can be brief — a callback, a parallel, a closing reflection that ties the personal and strategic together.

---

## 3. The Clean-Up Trap

**What happens:** The writer removes the weird, specific, odd details — the exact brand, the specific phrase someone used, the temperature, the thing that didn't happen — and replaces them with tidy generalizations.

**Check:** Are there at least 3 specific odd details in the draft? Details that only this person would know or mention?

**Fix:** Go back to the raw material. Find the most specific, unexpected details and put them back. The thing the kid said. The exact meeting that got cancelled. The brand of coffee. Specificity creates authenticity.

**Red flag:** Any sentence where you could swap in a different person's name and it would still work. That means it's too generic.

---

## 4. The Signposting Trap

**What happens:** The writer adds structural scaffolding: "Let me say that again," "Here's the thing," "This is important," "Let me be clear." These are presentation phrases, not writing phrases.

**Check:** Search the draft for: "Let me," "Here's the thing," "This is important," "I want to be clear," "Let me say that again." How many are there?

**Fix:** Delete them. If a sentence needs to land, write a better sentence — don't narrate its importance. Check the Voice Profile for whether the client actually uses any of these patterns (some people do — most don't).

---

## 5. The Parenthetical Trap

**What happens:** The writer cleans out mid-sentence qualifications, asides, and parenthetical thoughts into smooth, complete sentences. This removes the way the person actually thinks.

**Check:** Does the draft have any parenthetical asides? Mid-sentence qualifications? Honest admissions in brackets? If the Voice Profile says the client thinks in parentheses, there should be some.

**Fix:** Add them back where they fit naturally. "(honestly)", "(not to mention [specific detail])", "(don't tell anyone)" — these are thought patterns, not informality. They signal authenticity.

---

## 6. The Soft-Close Trap

**What happens:** The writer softens the commercial close because selling feels "off-brand." Instead of naming the offer and inviting directly, they hint and gesture vaguely toward "the work."

**Check:** Does the close name the specific offer, framework, or CTA? Or does it vaguely reference "going deeper" or "taking the next step"?

**Fix:** Check the Voice Profile and Offer Ecosystem. If the client is direct about their offers, be direct. Name the thing. Use the actual CTA language from the Offer Ecosystem. A direct invitation from an authentic voice is more honest than a softened hint.
