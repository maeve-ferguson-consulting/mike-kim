# Stress-Testing Protocol

Run this check on every draft before presenting. Two modes: defensive (is the draft honest?) and offensive (is the draft advancing the client's language?).

---

## Defensive Checks

These protect the client's credibility.

### 1. Is Every Claim Earned?

For every assertion in the draft, ask: "What earns this statement?"

- If it's a personal claim → it should trace to something from the raw material or IP Bank
- If it's a market claim → it should trace to intelligence or a known data point
- If it's a client result → it must be real, specific, and have permission to share

**Red flag:** Any sentence that starts with "Studies show..." or "Research suggests..." without a specific source. Either find the source or cut the claim.

### 2. Could Any Coach Write This?

Read each paragraph through the "only you" test. If you replaced the client's name with any other expert in the same space, would the paragraph still work?

If yes → it's too generic. The paragraph needs:
- A specific detail only this person would know
- A reference to their named framework or proprietary concept
- Their exact language (from the Voice Profile)
- A specific client story or proof point (from the IP Bank)

### 3. Does the Language Match?

Read the draft against the Voice Profile:
- Energy match? (Are you writing fast when they're slow? Sharp when they're warm?)
- Phrase match? (Did you use their signature phrases, or yours?)
- Anti-pattern check? (Did you fall into any of the traps their profile warns about?)
- Format match? (Did you use em-dashes when they prefer commas? Bullets when they prefer prose?)

### 4. AI-Speak Audit

Search for and eliminate:
- "In today's fast-paced world..."
- "Let's dive in..."
- "It's important to note..."
- "Here's the thing..."
- "At the end of the day..."
- "Unlock your potential..."
- "In conclusion..."
- Any phrase that sounds like it was generated rather than spoken

---

## Offensive Checks

These advance the client's positioning through language testing.

### 5. New Language Detection

When writing the draft, sometimes new phrases, framings, or names for things emerge — either from the raw material or from the natural flow of the writing.

When this happens:
1. **Flag it:** Note the new phrase in your presentation
2. **Weave it naturally:** Don't introduce it like a formal unveiling. Let it land mid-sentence like the client just said it
3. **Track it:** "I've stress-tested [new phrase] in this piece. See how it lands with your audience"

What to watch for in audience response:
- Gets highlighted or screenshot → strong resonance, candidate for IP Bank
- Gets replies or questions → resonates but needs clarification
- Gets shared → strong signal, add to signature phrases
- Gets no reaction → neutral, try it once more before dropping

### 6. Reinforce Before Innovating

Before introducing new language, check: are the client's existing signature phrases (from IP Bank and Voice Profile) well-represented?

Consistency builds association. If the audience hasn't fully absorbed the existing language, adding new phrases dilutes both. The rule: reinforce existing phrases first, introduce new ones sparingly. One new phrase per newsletter maximum.
